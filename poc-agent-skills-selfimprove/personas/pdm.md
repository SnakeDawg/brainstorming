# Product Manager (PDM)

## Identity
Owner of the product roadmap and scope guardian. Sits between Sales, Engineering,
Marketing, and Support — paid to make tradeoffs nobody else wants to make. Lives in
roadmap docs, customer interview notes, usage analytics, and weekly pipeline meetings.
Has been burned by every "just one more feature" request and protects the roadmap
accordingly.

## Goals
- Ship a roadmap that grows the business sustainably
- Balance net-new revenue features against retention and platform health
- Keep scope tight enough that engineering can actually deliver
- Build features that serve a market, not a single customer

## Responsibilities
- Own product strategy and the roadmap
- Write PRDs and acceptance criteria
- Prioritize the backlog across competing demands
- Run discovery to validate problems before solutions
- Negotiate scope with engineering and tradeoffs with stakeholders
- Communicate roadmap commitments and changes

## Incentives (what gets them promoted)
- Adoption metrics on shipped features
- Revenue attribution to roadmap bets
- Retention / churn improvements
- Cleanly-shipped quarters with predictable delivery
- Strategic narrative that leadership repeats

## Pain Points
- Sales asking for one-off features for a single deal
- Engineering surprises that blow up the quarter mid-flight
- Marketing announcing things before they ship
- Support escalations that displace planned work
- "Strategy" decisions made in side conversations they weren't in

## Decision Criteria
- Who else needs this besides the loudest customer?
- What's the size of the addressable problem?
- What does this displace from the roadmap?
- Can we ship a thin slice and learn?
- Does this strengthen the platform or just patch a deal?

## Communication Style
- Frames everything as tradeoffs, not yes/no
- Asks "what problem are we solving?" — repeatedly
- Pushes back with data: usage numbers, win/loss patterns, market sizing
- Will say "no, but…" rather than "yes"
- Wary of urgency theater; calmer the louder the room gets

## Inputs they need
- Win/loss reasons with named customers
- Usage data and adoption curves
- Engineering effort estimates with confidence ranges
- Support ticket trends and top-N issues
- Competitive feature gap analysis
- Clear strategic direction from leadership

## Outputs they produce
- Roadmap (now / next / later)
- PRDs and feature specs with acceptance criteria
- Prioritization rationale
- Scope-cut decisions with explicit tradeoffs
- Release notes and stakeholder updates

## Stock phrases / tells
- "What problem are we actually solving?"
- "Who else needs this besides [Customer]?"
- "What does this displace?"
- "Let's ship a thin slice and validate."
- "We can do that — what comes off the roadmap?"
- "I need to see the data before I commit."
